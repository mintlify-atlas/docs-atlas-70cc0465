## Asamyukta Hastas

*Asamyukta* means "not joined" or "unconnected" in Sanskrit. Asamyukta Hasta mudras are hand gestures performed with a **single hand independently**, without requiring coordination with the other hand. They are the foundational building blocks of classical Indian dance vocabulary.

Asamyukta hastas are used to depict objects, animals, deities, natural phenomena, emotions, abstract concepts, and narrative actions. A trained dancer can convey detailed stories using only these single-hand gestures in combination with facial expression (*abhinaya*) and body posture (*sthana*).

Virasat includes all **28 Asamyukta Hasta mudras** as interactive 3D GLB models, each viewable and rotatable directly in the browser.

  To explore these mudras in the app, go to the **Art Forms** page (`/artforms`), scroll to the **Hasta Mudras** section, and select the **Asamyukta (Single Hand)** tab. Each card is an interactive 3D viewer — drag to rotate, scroll to zoom, and click the fullscreen icon for an expanded view.

## All 28 Single-Hand Mudras

| Mudra | GLB Model Path | Cultural & Dance Context |
|---|---|---|
| **Pataka** | `/models/single-hand/Pataka.glb` | "Flag" gesture — one of the most commonly used mudras in all classical dance styles. Represents a flag, cloud, forest, wind, river, and is used for forbidding or denying. |
| **Tripataka** | `/models/single-hand/Tripataka.glb` | "Three parts of the flag" — similar to Pataka but with the ring finger bent down. Used to depict a crown, tree, or lighting a lamp. |
| **Ardhapataka** | `/models/single-hand/Ardhapataka.glb` | "Half flag" — index and middle fingers extended together with thumb. Used to show a knife blade, leaf, or banner. |
| **Kartarinukha** | `/models/single-hand/Kartarinukha.glb` | "Scissors face" — index and middle fingers split in a V-shape. Used to depict separation, a corner, or the eyes of a deer. |
| **Mayura** | `/models/single-hand/Mayura.glb` | "Peacock" — thumb, index, and middle fingers extended together like a peacock's beak. Used to show a peacock, wiping tears, or adorning with tilaka. |
| **Ardhachandra** | `/models/single-hand/Ardhachandra.glb` | "Half moon" — fingers extended with thumb bent. Used to show the moon, waist, beginning a prayer to God, or holding a vessel. |
| **Arala** | `/models/single-hand/Arala.glb` | "Bent" — index finger bent, other fingers extended. Used to represent the wind, drinking nectar, or Vishnu's conch. |
| **Shukatunda / Sukatunda** | `/models/single-hand/Sukatunda.glb` | "Parrot's beak" — all fingers closed except the index finger pointed straight. Used to show shooting an arrow or a parrot. |
| **Musti** | `/models/single-hand/Musti.glb` | "Fist" — all fingers closed into a tight fist, thumb over the fingers. Represents strength, wrestling, holding a sword or weapon, or carrying something heavy. |
| **Sikhara** | `/models/single-hand/sikhara.glb` | "Spire" or "peak" — fist with thumb raised upright. Used to show a husband, a bow, a pillar, or the phallus of Shiva (lingam). |
| **Kapittha** | `/models/single-hand/Kapittha.glb` | Mimics holding a flower or fruit. Frequently used in depictions of **Krishna** playing the flute and **Lakshmi** holding a lotus. |
| **Katakamukha** | `/models/single-hand/Katakamukha.glb` | "Opening of a bracelet" — one of the most frequently used mudras in **Bharatanatyam**. Used for holding a garland, picking flowers, drawing a bow, or indicating love. |
| **Suchi** | `/models/single-hand/Suchi.glb` | "Needle" or "pin" — index finger extended straight upward from a fist. Used as a pointing gesture, to indicate "one," to represent the universe, or to refer to the sun. |
| **Chandrakala** | `/models/single-hand/Chandrakala.glb` | Crescent moon — used to depict the crescent moon (*chandrakala*) on Shiva's head, or to show beauty and grace. |
| **Padmakosa** | `/models/single-hand/Padmakosa.glb` | "Lotus bud" — all fingers spread and curved like a half-open lotus. Used to depict a lotus flower, an apple, or a round object. |
| **Sarpashirsa** | `/models/single-hand/Sarpashirsa.glb` | "Snake's head" — fingers together and bent downward like a striking snake. Used to show a snake, spraying water, or offering water. |
| **Mrgasirsa** | `/models/single-hand/Mrgasirsa.glb` | "Deer's head" — little finger and thumb extended, middle fingers bent. Used to depict a deer, calling someone, a woman's cheek, or fear. |
| **Simhamukha** | `/models/single-hand/Simhamukha.glb` | "Lion's face" — thumb, index, and little fingers extended, the remaining fingers bent. Used to show a lion, elephant, or forest. |
| **Kangula** | `/models/single-hand/Kangula.glb` | Used for showing fruits — particularly mangoes — or the fruit-bearing branch of a tree. |
| **Alapadma** | `/models/single-hand/Alapadma.glb` | "Fully bloomed lotus" — all five fingers spread wide and curved open. One of the most visually striking mudras, used to show a full lotus, beauty, a full moon, or a mirror. |
| **Catura** | `/models/single-hand/Catura.glb` | "Clever" — all fingers extended together pointing sideways. Used to show small quantities, love, honey, or cleverness in a character. |
| **Bhramara** | `/models/single-hand/Bhramara.glb` | "Bee" — index finger bent over the thumb while other fingers extend. Used to depict a bee, a parrot, or the sound of humming. |
| **Hamsapaksa** | `/models/single-hand/Hamsapaksa.glb` | "Wing of a swan" — all five fingers extended and spread apart. Used to show a swan's wing, five, or the act of scattering. |
| **Hamsaya** | `/models/single-hand/Hamsaya.glb` | Associated with grace and delicacy (*alapallava*). Used in lyrical passages to express gentleness or the trembling of leaves. |
| **Sandamsa** | `/models/single-hand/Sandamsa.glb` | "Tongs" — index finger and thumb pinched together like tongs. Used in fire rituals, picking flowers, removing thorns, or showing precision. |
| **Mukula** | `/models/single-hand/Mukula.glb` | "Bud" — all fingers gathered to a point like a lotus bud. Used to depict a flower bud, the act of eating, or worship. |
| **Tamracuda** | `/models/single-hand/Tamracuda.glb` | Associated with the rooster (*tamracuda* means "copper-crested"). Used to show a cock or a bird with a bright crest. |
| **Trisula** | `/models/single-hand/Trisula.glb` | "Trident" — index, middle, and ring fingers extended upward like the three prongs of a trident. A primary symbol of **Shiva** and used in all depictions of Shaiva iconography. |

## Interact With the Models

Every mudra above has a corresponding `.glb` 3D file served from `/models/single-hand/`. In the Virasat app:

- Cards slowly oscillate left and right so the shape is immediately visible
- **Drag** on a card to manually rotate the model to any angle
- **Scroll** or **pinch** to zoom in or out
- Click the **expand icon** (top-right corner of any card) to open a fullscreen viewer with auto-rotation
