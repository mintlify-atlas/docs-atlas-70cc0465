## Samyukta Hastas

*Samyukta* means "joined" or "combined" in Sanskrit. Samyukta Hasta mudras are hand gestures that **require both hands working together** — either pressed against each other, interlocked, or positioned in coordinated relationship. These gestures generally carry greater symbolic weight than single-hand gestures and are frequently used for sacred, ritual, or narratively significant moments in performance.

Samyukta hastas appear in invocations, benedictions, depictions of deities and animals, and moments of high emotional or dramatic intensity. Many directly reference Hindu iconography: the Shiva lingam, Vishnu's Sudarshana chakra and Garuda vehicle, and the boar avatar Varaha all have dedicated Samyukta mudras.

Virasat includes all **22 Samyukta Hasta mudras** as interactive 3D GLB models. Two-hand models are rendered with the camera oriented at 90° to face the front of the joined gesture directly.

<Note>
  To explore these mudras in the app, go to the **Art Forms** page (`/artforms`), scroll to the **Hasta Mudras** section, and select the **Samyukta (Two Hands)** tab. Each card is an interactive 3D viewer — drag to rotate, scroll to zoom, and click the fullscreen icon for an expanded view.
</Note>

## All 22 Two-Hand Mudras

| Mudra | GLB Model Path | Cultural & Dance Context |
|---|---|---|
| **Anjali** | `/models/two-hand/anjali.glb` | "Salutation" — palms pressed flat together, fingers pointing upward. The universal gesture of greeting, prayer, and respect (*Namaste*). Opens most classical dance performances and is used in any moment of worship or reverence. |
| **Kapota** | `/models/two-hand/kapota.glb` | "Dove" or "pigeon" — hands cupped together with thumbs slightly parted, held at chest level. Used to receive blessings, show salutation, or depict respectful acceptance. |
| **Karkata** | `/models/two-hand/karkata.glb` | "Crab" — fingers of both hands interlocked and bent, forming a crab-like shape. Used to show a forest, the back of an elephant, or stretching the body. |
| **Svastika** | `/models/two-hand/svastika.glb` | Arms crossed at the wrists in front of the chest. Used to show a fish, the swastika auspicious symbol, or intertwining. |
| **Dola** | `/models/two-hand/Dola.glb` | "Swing" — both arms hang naturally at the sides of the body with relaxed hands. Used at the start of a dance or when standing naturally; represents ease and grace. |
| **Puspaputa** | `/models/two-hand/puspaputa.glb` | "Flower basket" — both hands cupped together, open like a vessel to hold flowers. Used to offer flowers, receive offerings, or show collecting water. |
| **Utsanga** | `/models/two-hand/Utsanga.glb` | Embracing oneself — each hand touches the opposite shoulder. Used to show modesty, shame, cold, or self-embrace. |
| **Sivalinga** | `/models/two-hand/Sivalinga.glb` | Directly represents the **Shiva lingam** — one hand cupped as the base (*yoni*) while the other forms an upright cylindrical shape above it. Used exclusively in Shaiva devotional contexts. |
| **Katakavardhana** | `/models/two-hand/Katakavardhana.glb` | A highly auspicious gesture used in **coronations**, marriages, and auspicious ceremonies. Both hands form the Katakamukha position facing each other. Used to show prosperity and blessings. |
| **Kartarisvastika** | `/models/two-hand/Kartarisvastika.glb` | Scissors crossed at the wrists — both hands form the Kartarinukha (scissors) gesture and cross at the wrists. Used to show the top of a tree, a tiger, or beauty. |
| **Sakata** | `/models/two-hand/Sakata.glb` | "Cart" or "chariot" — both fists held together. Used to represent a demon, a chariot, or a cart. |
| **Sankha** | `/models/two-hand/Sankha.glb` | "Conch shell" — one hand encircles the upright thumb of the other hand, mimicking a conch. The conch (*sankha*) is one of the principal symbols of **Vishnu** and is used to open sacred ceremonies. |
| **Chakra** | `/models/two-hand/Chakra.glb` | "Wheel" or "disc" — both index fingers extended and thumbs touching, forming a circular shape. Represents the **Sudarshana Chakra**, the spinning discus weapon of **Vishnu**. Featured on the Explore page. |
| **Pasa** | `/models/two-hand/Pasa.glb` | "Noose" or "rope" — fingers of both hands interlocked, representing a snare or rope. Used to depict capture, bondage, or the weapon of Yama, the god of death. |
| **Kilaka** | `/models/two-hand/Kilaka.glb` | "Pin" or "nail" — fingers interlocked with both index fingers extended and pointed outward. Used to show affection between a couple, a pin, or a bolt. |
| **Matsya** | `/models/two-hand/Matsya.glb` | "Fish" — one hand placed flat on the back of the other, both thumbs extended outward like fins. Used to show a fish or **Matsya**, the fish avatar of Vishnu. |
| **Kurma** | `/models/two-hand/Kurma.glb` | "Tortoise" — both hands cupped together with interlocked fingers, resembling a tortoise shell. Used to show **Kurma**, the tortoise avatar of Vishnu, or any tortoise. |
| **Varaha** | `/models/two-hand/Varaha.glb` | "Boar" — represents **Varaha**, the boar avatar of Vishnu who lifted the earth from the cosmic ocean. A significant mudra in Vaishnava devotional dance. |
| **Garuda** | `/models/two-hand/Garuda.glb` | "Eagle" — thumbs interlocked with fingers spread like wings, representing **Garuda**, the divine eagle and vehicle of Vishnu. Featured on the Explore page. |
| **Nagabandha** | `/models/two-hand/Nagabandha.glb` | "Serpent coil" — both arms crossed and hands pressed together to mimic coiling serpents. Used to show serpents intertwined or the nagas of Hindu mythology. |
| **Bherunda** | `/models/two-hand/Bherunda.glb` | Represents the *Bherunda*, a mythical two-headed bird of great power found in Indian iconography. Used in contexts depicting this creature or extraordinary strength. |
| **Katva** | `/models/two-hand/Katva.glb` | A cutting gesture — both hands used together to convey the act of cutting or separation. |

## Interact With the Models

Every mudra above has a corresponding `.glb` 3D file served from `/models/two-hand/`. In the Virasat app, two-hand models are initialized at a **90° camera angle** to face the joined gesture front-on, giving the clearest view of the combined hand shape.

- Cards slowly oscillate left and right around the 90° front-facing position
- **Drag** on a card to manually rotate the model to any angle
- **Scroll** or **pinch** to zoom in or out
- Click the **expand icon** (top-right corner of any card) to open a fullscreen viewer with auto-rotation
